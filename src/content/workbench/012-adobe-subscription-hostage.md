I like to think of myself as an idealist. I understand that businesses need to make money. I get that inflation is real, payroll is expensive, and keeping the lights on in a global infrastructure isn't free. I am not the guy who thinks everything should be cheap just because I want it to be. 

But I am also a guy who knows when I am being played.

Lately, the relationship between big software companies and their customers has started to feel less like a partnership and more like a hostage situation. Adobe is currently the poster child for this shift.

## The $30 Surprise

Let’s be fair: I knew I was on a promotion. Back in March of 2025, I signed up for a one year deal at $34.99 a month. That part is on me. I didn't set a calendar reminder, and I missed the window. If the bill had just gone back to the "normal" price, I might have just sighed and moved on.

But that isn't what happened. 

Right as my promo ended, Adobe decided to "enhance" my experience by rebranding my plan to "Creative Cloud Pro" and hiking the price to $69.99 a month. They sent a long email full of corporate-speak about "Firefly generative AI" and "collaborative mood boarding." 

The problem is, I didn't ask for "Pro." I’m a photographer who wanted to play with Premiere Pro on the side. I didn't need "Unlimited standard AI generations." I just needed the tools I was already using to keep working without a 100% price jump. There was no "Hey, your promo is ending next week" reminder. Just a "We’ve renamed your plan and it costs double now" notification.

## The Retention Dance

The most frustrating part of this wasn't the price jump itself. It was what happened when I clicked "Cancel." 

Suddenly, the algorithm decided I was worth the $35 price again. They immediately offered me the original deal to stay. This is where the trust breaks down. If you can afford to sell me the service for $35 today, why did you charge me $70 yesterday? 

It proves the price isn't based on the cost of the service or the "ongoing innovation" they mentioned in the email. It’s based on how much friction you’re willing to tolerate before you walk away. It turns a professional tool into a used car negotiation.

## The Cloud Storage Lockout

I decided to move on anyway. I just needed to do one quick task: convert a PDF to a JPG. Adobe offers a "free" tool for this if you’re logged in. 

Except I couldn't use it. 

Because I had been a paying customer for a year, my cloud storage was full of old projects. Now that I wasn't an "active" subscriber at the high tier, Adobe blocked the free utility because my storage was over the limit. 

Think about that logic. Because I gave them money for a year and used their service as intended, I am now locked out of basic, unrelated tools until I either delete my history or start paying the ransom again. My own data is being used as a weight to keep me from leaving the ecosystem. That isn't a feature. It’s anti-consumer friction.

## Shareholder Value vs. Consumer Value

We’ve reached a point where "increasing shareholder value" has become a euphemism for "how much can we annoy our users before they quit." 

We used to buy products because they were good. Now, it feels like we stay subscribed because it’s too painful to untangle our lives from the service. When did we stop focusing on making tools so excellent that users *want* to pay, and start focusing on making them so integrated that users *have* to pay?

## Voting with the Dollar

The good news is that we still have a choice. 

Look at what happened with Xbox Game Pass. They pushed the price too far in 2025, hitting that $30 mark for the Ultimate tier. The market pushed back. People like me cancelled. And in April of 2026, they actually walked it back. Under new leadership, they lowered the price because the churn was too high to ignore.

They listened because they had to.

I’m taking that same approach. I’ve realized I don't need the Adobe "Pro" tax. I’m sticking with Riverside.fm for my podcast recording and editing, and I’m moving over to DaVinci Resolve for my other video work. They provide the value I need without the hostage tactics.

If we want a better experience from companies like Adobe, or the next one on the list (I see you, Netflix), we have to be willing to walk away. If you want to charge a premium price, you have to provide a premium, friction-less experience. 

If the service isn't worth the cost, stop paying it. It’s the only vote they actually count.
